# Laboratory Journal: Exotic Avian & Culinary Eggs

## Ostrich Eggs (Struthio camelus)
- **Molecular Profile**: Extremely high phospholipid density in the yolk compared to Hen eggs.
- **Coagulation Kinetics**: Due to the massive volume (equivalent to 24 hen eggs), heat transfer is non-linear. The large yolk requires a sustained 64.5°C for at least 45 minutes to reach a "Jammy" consistency.
- **Protein Lattice**: The Ovomucin structure in Ostrich whites is significantly denser, leading to a much firmer set even at lower-end coagulation temperatures.

## Emu Eggs (Dromaius novaehollandiae)
- **Visual Identity**: Famous for their deep emerald/teal shells.
- **Yolk-to-White Ratio**: Significantly higher yolk-to-white ratio than chicken eggs.
- **Culinary Physics**: Emu yolk is exceptionally rich and creamy, with a lower water content. When whisked, it forms a lipid-stabilized foam that is resistant to collapse at 60°C.

## Century Eggs (Pidan / Pi Dan)
- **Biochemical Process**: An alkaline fermentation process (not true fermentation, but a chemical transformation) using salt, lime, and ash.
- **Molecular Transformation**: The pH of the egg rises to 9-12. This causes the proteins to denature and cross-link into a translucent, dark brown jelly.
- **The "Pine Branch" Patterns**: Dendritic crystals of calcium hydroxide form on the surface, indicating high-purity protein breakdown.

## Salted Duck Eggs
- **Osmotic Transition**: Brining forces salt into the yolk, which draws out water and causes the lipids to aggregate into a "grainy" or "oily" texture.
- **Safety Protocol**: Always ensure a 1:4 salt-to-water ratio for at least 30 days to ensure complete osmotic saturation and bacteria inhibition.
